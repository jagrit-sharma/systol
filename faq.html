Frequently asked questions

Systol is a live heart-rate monitor that runs entirely in your browser. Here's how it works, what it can and can't do, and how your data is handled.

Not a medical device

Systol is a hobbyist, educational project, not a certified medical device. Don't rely on it for diagnosis, treatment, or any decision about your health. Readings come entirely from your consumer sensor and can be inaccurate, delayed, or missing.

What is Systol?

Connect a Bluetooth heart-rate sensor and Systol shows your live BPM, a scrolling trace of the last 60 seconds, which heart-rate zone you're in, and how long you've spent in each zone this session. It's built to feel like a bedside monitor, right in a browser tab.

Is Systol free?

Yes, completely. There's no charge, no account to create, no sign-in, no subscription, and no paid tier holding features back: everything you see is the whole app. There are no ads, no analytics, and no tracking of any kind, which is straightforward to guarantee because Systol has no server to send anything to. It's open source under the MIT License, so you're also free to read the code, modify it, or run your own copy.

Which browsers work?

Systol uses the Web Bluetooth API, which only Chromium-based browsers support: Chrome, Edge, Opera, Brave, and similar, on desktop and Android. Firefox and Safari have chosen not to implement it, so Systol can't connect to a device there. (On iPhone and iPad, no browser supports it: that's an Apple platform limitation, not a browser choice.)

What devices can I connect?

Any Bluetooth Low Energy sensor that advertises the standard Heart Rate service (0x180D): most chest straps (Polar, Garmin, Wahoo, and others) and some armbands and smartwatches.

Why won't my Fitbit (or watch) connect, or show battery?

Many fitness wearables, including Fitbit, don't expose the standard Bluetooth Heart Rate service to other apps; they keep the data inside their own ecosystem. If a device doesn't advertise 0x180D, Systol simply can't see its heart rate. Battery is a separate, optional service (0x180F); when a device doesn't offer it, the battery indicator just stays hidden.

Why don't I see my sensor's battery level?

Because that sensor doesn't report one. Battery is an optional Bluetooth service (0x180F), separate from heart rate, and plenty of straps simply don't publish it. Systol asks for it once when you connect: if the sensor answers, the battery chip appears in the header and a Battery row appears in the device panel; if it doesn't, both stay hidden rather than showing an empty value. Nothing is wrong with your sensor or your connection, and heart rate works exactly the same either way. If you see a dash (--) instead, that's just the placeholder shown before a device is connected.

Do I have to pick my device every time I connect?

For now, yes: each time you connect, your browser shows its Bluetooth picker and you choose your sensor. That's the browser's own security model: a page only gets access to a device you explicitly select. One-tap reconnect to a remembered sensor is planned for a future update.

Will my session be saved if I refresh or close the tab?

Not yet. Systol keeps your current session in memory only, so refreshing the page, closing the tab, or navigating away starts a fresh session. If you want to keep a workout, export it first (Settings → Session data → Export CSV/JSON). Saved session history is on the roadmap.

What does "Signal lost" mean?

It's a connection notice, not a health alarm

The "Signal lost" pill means Systol stopped receiving readings from your sensor. It may have disconnected, moved out of range, run low on battery, or lost skin contact. It says nothing about you; only that the data stream stopped. That's also why it plays no sound.

What does "No pulse detected" mean?

Some sensors keep sending data but report a heart rate of zero when they can't find a pulse: a strap resting on a table, dry electrodes, or a wrist sensor lifted off the skin. Systol treats that honestly: it holds your last real reading, freezes the heart, and shows a "No pulse detected" notice rather than drawing a fake flatline or a misleading number. Recovery is instant on the next real beat.

How do heart-rate zones work?

Each reading is sorted into Light, Moderate, Vigorous, or Peak by BPM thresholds. You can set your own in Settings → Heart rate zones (defaults: Moderate 115, Vigorous 135, Peak 160). The big number, the heart, and the trace all recolor to your current zone.

What are target-zone alerts?

Choose a target zone in Settings → Alerts, and Systol flashes, and optionally chimes, when you enter or leave it. A brief hold before it fires keeps alerts from flickering when you hover right on a zone boundary.

What is monitor mode?

A fullscreen, glanceable view: a giant BPM readout and a large trace with the rest of the interface hidden, like a bedside monitor. Enter it from the header button (or the expand button on the chart) and leave it with Esc.

Can I hear my heartbeat?

Turn on Beep on each beat in Settings → Audio and a soft tick plays in time with your live heart rate, with a few tones to choose from. Browsers require one click or tap on the page before any sound can start.

How do I export my session?

Open Settings → Session data and choose Export CSV or Export JSON. The file downloads straight from your browser; nothing is uploaded.

Is my data private?

Yes. Everything happens on your device: there's no server and no network calls, so your readings are never uploaded. Your settings (theme, zones, and preferences) are saved in your browser's local storage on this device, and exported files are generated locally in the browser. Nothing is ever sent anywhere.

What's demo mode?

Add ?demo to the address (for example …/?demo) to run Systol with simulated heart-rate data, no device required. It's handy for trying the app out or previewing every state without a sensor.
